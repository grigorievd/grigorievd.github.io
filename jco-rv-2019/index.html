ART LEADS JAPAN’S REGIONAL
REVITALIZATION

Art and art festivals have prompted the emergence of an “arty-pelago” in Japan and have shown how art can help revitalize communities and promote development.

ARTS AT THE HELM
FOR REGIONAL
REVITALIZATION

Like Europe, Japan faces a declining birth rate and an aging society, leading to a falling population. In recent decades, industry has disappeared from some of its rural areas, while more young people have moved to major cities in search of opportunities.

Despite this, rural communities are making the most of their areas' unique features to build vibrant and sustainable societies. Art festivals are a key aspect of this effort.

“It is important to realize the uniqueness of each region and appreciate its value. This is how art reinvigorates and revitalizes local communities,” says KITAGAWA Fram, President of Art Front Gallery.

Art has been identified as having an important role to play for regional revitalization. Visitors to Japan’s many arts festivals are able to enjoy several unique experiences that include world-class art, local cuisine and culture and that charming undisputed Japanese hospitality.

With external funding, sponsorships and hard work from artistic groups, local communities have become more involved in turning the spotlight on the natural beauty, cultural wealth and creativity of their communities.

A series of festivals, residencies, and new architectural structures has successfully driven recent cultural tourism in Japan, ultimately leading to both the repopulation and economic rebirth of remote areas.

  • Echigo-Tsumari Art Triennale 2018

    An increase in regional employment was seen as a result of the event: a total of 662 people was added to the workforce.

  • Setouchi Triennale 2016

    7000 volunteers participated in the event which included professionals from corporations, university students, seniors.

  • Okayama Art Summit 2016

  • Oku-Noto Triennale 2017

    After its debut, the number of tourist increased 160% from the previous year.

ARTS
AS THE FUNDAMENTAL
CATALYST FOR
REJUVENATION

Some of the most groundbreaking art festivals in Japan have contributed to the regeneration of its countryside while showcasing the resourcefulness and spirit of its people.

01

Echigo-Tsumari
Art Triennale

For Lots of Lost Windows
— UTSUMI Akiko
Deep in the mountains of Niigata Prefecture, the Echigo-Tsumari Art Triennale (ETAT), which first took place in 2000, appears every three years to become the world's largest international art festival, and covers 300 square miles – including an open-air 'art field'.

Since its introduction, the Triennale has attracted 2.6 million people. Over a hundred of the world's best artists display their artworks in villages that line the region’s mountains and terraced rice fields. Visitors can embark on an art-guided journey that promises a rediscovery of one’s relationships with nature, art, and humanity.

  • Trees — Menashe Kadishman
  • Terraced rice fields in Tokamachi
  • The Rice Field — Ilya & Emilia Kabakov
  • Tsumari in Bloom — KUSAMA Yayoi
  • Ubusuna House, Tokamachi, Niigata
  • Tokamachi, Niigata
  • Café Reflet — Jean-Luc Vilmouth

The Triennale takes these stunning works of art out of their traditional urban settings and temporarily houses them in the region’s forgotten spaces. Once-abandoned houses and schools have been transformed into vital parts of the local community, injecting zest into contemporary life.

One such example is the Museum of Picture Book Art, which gave the shuttered Sanada Elementary School a new lease on life. TASHIMA Seizo, the famed picture book artist and writer, led the project, and visitors can experience the story of the last three children that attended the school through the walk-in picture book.

MIGRANT’S STORY

One of the festival’s fringe events saw international artists and local volunteers collaborating to give back to land by planting vegetable seeds, tending rice crops and looking for ways to encourage water sustainability.

One local resident who has seen the benefits of ETAT first-hand is TAKAGI Chiho. She moved from Tokyo in 2011 and now runs a craft beer business. She is grateful for the opportunities brought to the community by the festival.

“The number of people who come here for the art festival and buy Tsumari beer has increased,” she said. “I think it shows how much recognition Echigo-Tsumari has, and I hope to provide visitors with a chance to touch local products made of local ingredients and keep telling them the meaning and value of local things.”

JAPAN’S
ART BEATS

There are several other arts festivals bringing new life to rural regions of Japan as well. For example, the below two festivals aim to bring new economic and creative life to regions impacted by the Great East Japan Earthquake of 2011.

Yamagata Biennale

Created in 2014 and hosted on the grounds of the Tohoku University of Art and Design, the Yamagata Biennale celebrates Japan and its language through exhibits of cutting-edge creativity across art mediums while showcasing locally inspired projects alongside breakthrough pieces.

Reborn-Art Festival

Set against the picturesque Oshika Peninsula and Ishinomaki City in Miyagi Prefecture and first held in 2017, this festival brings music, art, food and the vibrant local creative scene together.

THE WAY FORWARD

That Japan is seeking a constructive resolution to overcome its population challenges its urban areas is expected. How the country is doing this, however, is inspiring.

Government policies are, of course, important, but out-the-box thinking is required as well.

This is where visionary artists and local residents play a critical role. They have devised innovative platforms to regenerate Japan’s rural countryside, allowing residents to once again be proud of where they live, and visitors to explore Japan’s many wonderful, yet perhaps overlooked, rural areas.
