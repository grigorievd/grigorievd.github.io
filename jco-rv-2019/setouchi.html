Setouchi Triennale

Chinu – The Black Sea Bream of Uno
— Yodogawa Technique

Elsewhere in 2019, the Setouchi Triennale hosted its fourth edition, in an ongoing effort to revitalize a region devastated by economic changes and shifting demographics.

The event, which started in 2010, breathes new life into the beautiful islands of the Seto Inland Sea. Cocooned by three of Japan’s four main islands, this area is the perfect backdrop for art. Set against a stunning landscape and calm blue waters, visitors are first greeted by a warm light and in the distance, 3,000 island peaks can be spotted on the horizon.

One of Japan’s biggest and most influential art festivals, it celebrates Japanese contemporary art both along the coast and in the abandoned homes of villages. The featured artworks echo the beauty of Japanese rural life and are a reflection of the island’s history. These islands are a textbook example of how art, architecture and nature come together to form an inspiring effect.

One of the artworks that best reflects this is the Inujima Seirensho Art Museum that has been creatively built into the abandoned ruins of an aged copper refiner, “using what exists to create what is to be", minimizing impact on the natural environment.

A local resident who has been inspired by the festival is KISHIMOTO Hitoshi. Ever since his return to Shodo-shima in 2010, the same year as the first Seoutchi Triennale, he’s participated in every iteration of the festival.

MIGRANT’S STORY

Kishimoto runs a restaurant called Koyomi, which came about through a project he initiated which linked local authors, such as TSUBOI Sakae, to the island’s food industry. The restaurant is hyper-local, and Kishimoto sources nearly all of his ingredients from Shodo-shima.

The island’s most notable ingredients include olives, soy sauce, somen and sesame oil, all of which are served at Koyomi.

“I want to fully use the island’s products, not only because they are delicious, but also because we’d like to serve cuisine unique to this island to make visitors come back again,” Mr. Kishimoto says. “In fact, there are customers who have already said they’ve come multiple times.”

Other new migrants to the region have also moved into previously derelict homes and converted them into guest houses, galleries, bars, baths, and cafes to cater to visitors, whose numbers peak during the festival.
