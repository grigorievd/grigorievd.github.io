The AI Influence on the Future of India

The Artificial Intelligence (AI) revolution is brewing in India. If leveraged, AI can potentially add US$957 billion (15 percent of current gross value) to India’s economy in 2035, says an Accenture report. Therefore, it’s imperative to harness AI to drive intelligent automation, boost workforces and propel innovation across sectors. And this is where policy makers and business leaders play critical roles.

If you are using a smartphone, you are interacting with AI whether you are aware or not. From the built-in smart assistants to the portrait mode in the camera, AI is impacting our lives every day.

When you are using music and media streaming services, AI is making the decisions for you. Next time you are hitting play on a recommended video or listening to a pre-created playlist, remember that AI is playing a big role in that.

If you are using social media, most of your decisions are being impacted by artificial intelligence. From the feeds that you see in your timeline to the notifications that you receive from these apps, everything is curated by AI.

Many large tech companies are investing in drone delivery programs and it will become a reality far sooner than what you expect. If you think that’s far-fetched, do note that militaries all over the world are already using successful drone programs.

The banking and finance industry heavily relies on artificial intelligence for things like customer service, fraud protection, and more. An example is the automated emails that you receive from banks whenever you do an out of the ordinary transaction.

Government
direction and
investment in
new tech and AI

While the Indian Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology in conjunction with Nasscom has created a “policy group” to design a roadmap for emerging technologies, the Ministry of Commerce and Industry has put together a “task force on AI” to study how AI solutions can work across sectors. The National Institution for Transforming India (NITI Aayog), the nation’s think-tank and premier policy-making body has outlined that AI in India will:

  • Contribute to growth by overcoming limitations of capital and labor

  • Improve the quality of life
    in the social sector

  • Drive the setting-up of “tinkering labs” so spin-offs of AI technologies can be developed for other countries

India’s AI journey has taken off on the back of the successful Digital India programme. With the Indian government allocating $480 million in 2018-19 to research, training and skill development in – AI, digital manufacturing, robotics, Quantum communication and Big Data intelligence, 3D printing, Blockchain, Machine Learning and Internet of Things – new technologies are directing the development of the economy, society and human resources.

Distribution of Artificial Intelligence companies

Global India

Part of this AI-based national strategy also ensures focus on: Health, Education, Agriculture, Smart Cities and Mobility – sectors chosen based on successful interventions globally and their potential for high social impact in India. To this end, the NITI Aayog is developing a National Data and Analytics Portal to facilitate training and dataset-sharing between stakeholders for AI-related applications.

Despite the promise this holds, India lags behind other G20 countries due to lack of funding, inadequate research, the public private collaboration and insufficient deployment of services from private sector players.

% of AI professionals by work experience in India

Around 2,400 fresher were added to AI workforce in India in 2017.

Almost 55% AI professionals in India have a work experience less than 5 years.

The average work experience of AI professionals in India is 6.6 years.

Its difficult to estimate the size of the AI sector in India given many applications are at a nascent stage. But the Indian AI sector has grown between 2012 and 2017 with $150 million invested across 400 companies. Investments have doubled from $44 million in 2016 to $77 million in 2017.

Education and
future AI
technologies

Such investments have spurred talent and innovation in India since 2016. The 2.6 million graduates with STEM skills and the talent to build AI technologies however have been handicapped by their low employability which has mandated re-skilling according to the demands of AI.

AI Research driven largely by society’s needs has faced hurdles in the form of language barriers and the presence of substantial numbers of the disadvantaged. It will take time to navigate these issues.

However, the government is establishing different hubs focusing on myriad areas of research at – the Indian Institutes of Technology (IITs), speech processing units at the Tata Institute of Fundamental Research, parallel processing at the Indian Institute for Science, image processing at the Indian Statistical Institute and natural language processing at the Center for Development of Advanced Computing – in a bid to constructively boost education and economic growth.
